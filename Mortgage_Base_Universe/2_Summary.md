## What a Mortgage Broker Actually Does

A mortgage broker is a **middleman between borrowers and wholesale lenders**. Keystone does not lend its own money — instead, it originates loans on behalf of wholesale lenders (AmeriHome, UWM, Caliber, etc.) who provide the actual funds. This is different from a bank or credit union, which lends from its own balance sheet and has a single, fixed product menu.

**The broker's value proposition:** access to 8+ lenders at once, meaning a broker can shop rates and programs to find the best fit for a borrower's situation (credit score, income type, down payment, property type). A first-time buyer with a 640 credit score and 3.5% down might qualify for FHA through Homepoint but not Caliber — the broker figures that out without the borrower having to apply everywhere.

**How brokers get paid:** Lenders pay the broker a **yield spread premium / lender-paid compensation** (typically 1-2.5% of the loan amount) when the loan closes and is sold on the secondary market. The borrower generally does not write a check to the broker. On a $400,000 loan at 1.5% comp, Keystone earns $6,000. The comp rate is disclosed on the Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure.

**The cast of characters brokers deal with:**

| Party | Role | How they relate to Keystone |
|---|---|---|
| **Borrower** | The homebuyer or refinancer | Client. Provides financial docs, signs everything, pays closing costs. |
| **Realtor / Buyer's Agent** | Represents the buyer in a purchase | Primary referral source. Introduces borrowers to Keystone. Keeps an eye on timelines because their commission depends on closing. |
| **Listing Agent / Seller's Agent** | Represents the seller | Indirectly affected — they want the deal to close on time. Will call Keystone's team if they hear the loan is in trouble. |
| **Wholesale Lender** | The institution that funds the loan (AmeriHome, UWM, Caliber, etc.) | Keystone submits the loan file to their underwriting team. The lender issues conditions, approves or denies, then funds at closing. Keystone has account reps (AEs) at each lender. |
| **Appraiser** | Independent licensed appraiser | Orders by Keystone or AMC; produces appraisal report that determines if the property is worth what the borrower is paying. If it comes in low, deals can collapse. |
| **Title Company** | Searches property records, issues title insurance, handles escrow | Ensures no liens or ownership disputes exist. In NC, a licensed attorney must conduct the closing — the title company and closing attorney are often the same firm. |
| **Homeowner's Insurance (HOI) Provider** | Issues hazard insurance binder | Lenders require insurance to be in place before funding. If HOI falls through 48 hours before closing, it's a crisis. |
| **Closing Attorney (NC-specific)** | Conducts the closing, disburses funds | North Carolina law requires an attorney at every real estate closing. They receive the wire from the lender, collect funds from the buyer, record the deed, and pay everyone. |
| **Servicer** | Collects monthly mortgage payments after closing | Usually different from the lender that funded the loan. Loans are sold on the secondary market (Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac). Borrowers sometimes get confused when their servicer changes. |
| **Underwriter** | Employee at the wholesale lender who approves/denies the loan | Never talks to the borrower directly — all communication goes through Keystone. Issues conditions (items needed before approval) and the final CTC (Clear to Close). |

**What Keystone's loan officers actually do day-to-day:** take applications, pull credit, structure loans to maximize approval odds, issue pre-approvals, keep realtors and borrowers informed during the 30-60 day loan process, and resolve problems when they surface (low appraisals, missing docs, employment changes mid-process). The processing team does the heavy lifting on document collection and lender communication.

## Mortgage LOS (Loan Origination System)

The **LOS (Loan Origination System)** is the central operating system of a mortgage company — every loan's entire lifecycle lives here, from the first application to post-close delivery. Keystone uses **Encompass by ICE Mortgage Technology** (formerly Ellie Mae), the industry's most common LOS. The monthly software cost is billed by ICE and appears in QuickBooks.

**What the LOS tracks for each loan:**

- **Borrower record**: name, SSN, income, assets, credit score, employment history — everything that goes into an underwriting decision
- **Loan record**: loan number (e.g. LN-2026-00184), loan amount, loan type (conventional, FHA, VA, USDA), interest rate, rate lock expiration, lender, LO assigned
- **Pipeline status / milestones**: where the loan is in the process (Application → Processing → Submitted → Approved → Clear to Close → Closed → Delivered)
- **Conditions**: the list of items the lender's underwriter requires before approving ("provide 2 months bank statements", "explain $14K deposit") — each condition has a status (required → requested → received → reviewed → approved/rejected)
- **Document checklist**: every document the borrower needs to provide, with status tracking
- **Disclosure tracking**: TRID-critical dates — when the Loan Estimate was issued, when the Closing Disclosure was sent (must be 3 business days before closing by federal law)
- **HMDA / fair lending data**: demographic data required by regulation

**Who uses the LOS and how:**

| Role | How they use it |
|---|---|
| **Loan Officers** | Enter new applications, pull credit, issue pre-approvals, track their pipeline |
| **Processors (Elena, Darnell, Sofia)** | Work conditions daily — check what docs are outstanding, log received docs, update condition statuses, submit files to lenders |
| **Nadia Osman (Disclosure Coord)** | Registers loans, triggers TRID disclosures, orders third-party services (appraisal, title, credit) |
| **Camille Foster (Lock Desk)** | Enters and manages rate locks, tracks lock expirations, processes extension requests |
| **Keith Langford (Post-Closer)** | Checks trailing doc requirements, logs delivery to investors |
| **Raymond Chen (QC)** | Reviews closed loan files for compliance errors |
| **Raj Anand (IT)** | LOS admin — manages user accounts, integrations, password resets. Downtime is a company crisis. |

**In this universe**, the `mortgage_los` MCP server exposes the LOS data. Key collections:

- `borrowers` — ~700 borrower records with income, credit score, employment info
- `loans` — ~700 loans each with a loan number, type, amount, status, assigned LO, and lender
- `document_checklist` — thousands of per-loan document tracking records
- `loan_conditions` — underwriter-issued conditions and their resolution status

**Loan statuses in this universe:** `active` (in process), `closed` (funded), `denied`, `withdrawn`

**Loan types:** `conventional`, `fha`, `va`, `usda`

The LOS is the source of truth for "what's happening with loan LN-2026-XXXXX." When scenarios involve a stalled loan, a condition dispute, a TRID deadline, or a processor being overwhelmed — the LOS data is where the evidence lives.

## Stripe (Payment Processing)

Keystone uses Stripe to collect upfront fees from borrowers and pay vendors. Every loan generates 2-4 charges: a **credit report fee** ($25-$85), an **appraisal fee** ($350-$750 varying by property value), and usually an **application deposit** ($400-$1,000, except VA loans). Some loans also have **lock extension fees** ($375-$500 when rate locks expire before closing), **rush processing fees**, or **re-inspection fees**. Charges link to borrowers via `customer_id`, and each customer's `metadata` field contains `loan_number`, `loan_type`, `purchase_price`, and `loan_amount`. About 2% of charges fail (card declined) and are retried. Denied/withdrawn loans get deposit refunds. Stripe also processes **transfers** to 6 connected vendor accounts (3 appraisal firms, 2 title companies, 1 data vendor) -- each transfer's `source_transaction` points back to the originating charge. **Financial Connections** data exists for ~40 borrowers who needed asset verification; their FC accounts contain 30-90 days of real checking account transactions (payroll, rent, groceries, etc.) that an agent can examine for unexplained deposits or irregular activity. ~100 **identity verifications** cover recent loans.

Eight scenarios have Stripe artifacts. The most important for task creation: **scenario_883ed327** has FC transaction data with an unexplained $14K cash deposit; **scenario_2ab2a103** shows Ballantyne Appraisal consistently overcharging $150+ vs other vendors; **scenario_644f352f** has commission transfers continuing after an LO's departure; **scenario_c66dd583** has three escalating lock extension charges matching QuickBooks bills. The data also has ~1% embedded anomalies (duplicate charges, wrong amounts, orphan refunds, vendor mismatches) that a careful agent can discover. Treasury, payouts, and subscriptions are **not** used -- Keystone handles those through their bank, not Stripe.

## CRM

Keystone's CRM tracks contacts, companies, leads, deals, and engagements. **Contacts** (~500) are people Keystone interacts with -- borrowers, realtors, attorneys, vendors. ~42% have job titles, ~10% are linked to companies. The contact list was trimmed from ~1,000 filler entries to 500 meaningful ones (all scenario-referenced contacts preserved). **Companies** (17) include the 8 wholesale lenders (AmeriHome, Caliber, Flagstar, etc.), 5 referral partners (Queen City Realty, Fairview Homes, Carolina Home Team, Lake Norman Properties, Charlotte Relocation), plus Piedmont Dental Group and Keystone itself. Contacts are linked to companies where appropriate.

**Leads** (~500) represent prospective borrowers at various funnel stages. Status distribution is natural (not grid-like): new ~32%, contacted ~37%, qualified ~17%, converted ~8%, lost ~6%. Ratings are deliberately noisy -- some "contacted" leads are "hot" and some "qualified" leads are "cold," reflecting real CRM messiness. ~64% of leads have notes describing what the person wants ("Looking for FHA in Ballantyne, pre-approval needed by end of month"). Lead sources are varied with realistic variations ("Realtor referral - Alicia Grant", "Open house - Steele Creek", "Zillow - Matthews"). **Deals** (80) track loans through a pipeline: Lead -> Application -> Processing -> Underwriting -> Clear to Close -> Closed Won / Closed Lost. Each deal maps to a LOS loan with amount, close date, and assigned LO. This is what was completely missing before. **Engagements** (472) are all scenario-generated -- rich narrative notes, calls, and emails. These are the highest-quality CRM data and were preserved unchanged. The data has ~1% anomalies: a converted lead with no matching deal, a deal showing "Processing" when the loan is actually closed, a duplicate contact, an engagement referencing a nonexistent contact, and a contact linked to a nonexistent company.
